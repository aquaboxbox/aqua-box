Features and Development

Hangar and Plane

The hangar environment and the plane displayed in the lobby were modelled using Blender. The original plan was to position the camera at various angles around the hangar to create distinct scenes for the main menu, credits, and lobby. However, due to time constraints, the hangar ultimately served only as the lobby environment, leaving much of the geometry behind the plane out of view.

Procedural Terrain

Several approaches were considered for terrain generation. Initially, a triangular binary tree approach was implemented, but due to time constraints, we switched to a geometry clipmap-based approach. In this setup, a pre-defined LOD mesh follows the player, updating all vertices dynamically on the GPU. The heightmap was generated by layering Brownian noise, with each layer's gradients adjusted to simulate natural erosion. To create a canyon, the squared distance from a dampened oscillating sine wave was applied multiple times, producing a “stepped” effect reminiscent of geological formations like the Grand Canyon.

Water Shader

The water effect was achieved through a custom shader that combines depth-based colour blending, dynamic UV offset from a flow map, and foam textures using procedural Voronoi noise. Sampling the camera depth texture transition colours from shallow to deep, while vertex displacement simulates subtle waves. Foam effects are layered by mixing light and dark shades of Voronoi noise, with anti-aliasing smoothing these foam edges and reducing detail at a distance, ensuring optimised rendering for far-off views.

Clouds

The clouds use a full screen render pass which uses raymarching, 3D worley/perlin noise and Beer's law in order to render volumetric clouds. This implementation has the benefit of being able to enter the clouds where the cloud density depends on the camera's position within the cloud.

Fire

The fire effect was created by intersecting three quads. Two of these quads used a fire shader program, while the third represented a burn mark positioned on the plane's wing. The fire shader is used for the fire on the plane's wing, adding an extra element of danger for the players to deal with.

Fuel Particle System

The fuel particle system was designed to visually link the collectible rings in the game to the plane's fuel supply. After exploring various options, we chose an effect that appeared in world space. Particles would emit evenly around the collection point and move toward the fuel bar. Animation curves were applied to adjust the speed and movement characteristics of the particles, adding a unique personality to the animation.

Character Design

The player characters' concepts went through many iterations. The design that stuck was the idea of a little bearded gnome-like character. While creating initial models the idea of “flightless birds” also came up, and after some iterations we knew what we had to stick with. The final design is based on a kakapo, a flightless parrot species endemic to New Zealand.

Shell Texturing

Shell texturing was something we knew we had to include in the player characters design from the beginning; initially developed by Lengyel (2000), our implementation adapts Garrett Gunnell's (Acerola) simplified Unity version. The technique works by duplicating a mesh multiple times to form concentric “shells” scaled outward along vertex normals. Each shell samples a noise or fur texture, with an alpha threshold discarding parts of the outer shells to create fur-like density. This stacking effect makes fur appear soft from a distance. Although efficient, artifacts can appear at model edges where shell separation is visible at glancing angles.

Networking and Interaction

Our networking and interaction solution connects player phones to a central screen via a WebGL scene: phones access the game through a web browser, where they use a generated join code to connect through the Unity Relay service. Player input, including gyroscope data for movement, is handled on the phone, which updates the player's movement and rotation and sends it to the central screen in real-time. The server manages player connections, assigns colours through a perceptual-based colour model, and handles and maintains player data, ensuring smooth updates and synchronisation across the networked environment.

Goals and Motivation

From the outset, we aimed to create a unique multiplayer experience using smartphones and a shared public screen. Inspired by last year's Peak Panic project, we were excited about building something similar with a fresh take. After an intensive brainstorming phase, we landed on developing a collaborative multiplayer game. We prioritised two main interaction concepts:

  1. Connecting multiple players via smartphone on a shared screen
  2. Incentivising teamwork and collaboration

On the graphics side, our team had diverse interests we wanted to explore, and Peak Panic, with its relatively simple gameplay, offered the perfect canvas. From the start, we identified key areas to focus on:

  • LOD-based Terrain Generation
  • Ray-Traced Cloud Generation
  • Procedural Character Animation
  • Shell Texturing
  • Advanced Water Shaders

The project naturally grew in scope as development progressed, but we successfully integrated these core elements that initially captivated our interest.

Obstacles and Challenges

The development of Peak Panic! presented numerous challenges, both expected and unexpected. For us one of the earliest hurdles in the development of the game was the very expected challenge of the networking framework. Even with guidance from the Peak Panic team, networking proved to be complex. We encountered additional technical limitations when working with WebGL and mobile browsers. Many smartphones, particularly iPhones, posed issues: WebGL lacks support for input fields on mobile browsers, and obtaining permissions for gyroscope data proved problematic.
